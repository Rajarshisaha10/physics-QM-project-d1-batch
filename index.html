Young's Double Slit Experiment

The Young’s Double Slit Experiment is a famous physics experiment first performed by Thomas Young in 1801, which demonstrates the wave nature of light. In the experiment, a beam of light passes through two closely spaced slits, producing an interference pattern (alternating bright and dark fringes) on a screen

Historical Background

The Revolutionary Experiment: In 1801, Thomas Young's double-slit experiment fundamentally changed our understanding of light and laid the groundwork for quantum mechanics.

1801

Thomas Young's Original Experiment

Young demonstrated the wave nature of light by showing interference patterns when light passed through two closely spaced slits. This challenged Newton's particle theory of light.

1805

Publication and Initial Reception

Young published his findings, initially met with skepticism from the scientific community that favored Newton's corpuscular theory.

1909

Single Photon Experiments

Geoffrey Taylor showed that even single photons create interference patterns, revealing the quantum nature of light.

1961

Electron Double-Slit

Claus Jönsson demonstrated interference with electrons, proving that matter also exhibits wave properties.

Thomas Young's experiment was revolutionary because it provided clear evidence that light behaves as a wave. At the time, Sir Isaac Newton's particle theory of light was widely accepted, making Young's wave theory controversial. The experiment showed that when light passes through two parallel slits, it creates an interference pattern on a screen - something that only waves can do.
